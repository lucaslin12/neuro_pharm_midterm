Key Concepts

  • Action Potential Generation: Action potentials rely on precise ionic movements, predominantly Na+ influx for depolarization and K+ efflux for repolarization. These steps are controlled by voltage-gated ion channels.
  • Ohm's Law in Neuronal Function: Ohm's law (V=IR) explains how voltage, current, and resistance interact within neuronal circuits. This relationship is key to understanding signal propagation.
  • Sodium-Potassium Pump: Essential for maintaining the resting membrane potential, the pump actively transports 3 Na+ ions out and 2 K+ ions into the neuron.
  • Refractory Periods: The absolute refractory period ensures unidirectional AP propagation by inactivating Na+ channels. The relative refractory period follows, requiring stronger stimuli to initiate another AP.
  • Capacitance: Higher capacitance slows voltage changes, impacting AP speed. This property depends on the size and structure of the neuron.
  • Electrochemical Gradients: These gradients drive ion flow, combining concentration and electrical forces to determine the movement of ions across the membrane.
  • Excitation vs. Inhibition: Excitation (e.g., glutamate signaling) depolarizes the membrane, while inhibition (e.g., GABA signaling) hyperpolarizes it, modulating neuronal activity.
  • Saltatory Conduction: Myelinated axons enable faster AP propagation via saltatory conduction, where APs "jump" between nodes of Ranvier.
  • Ion Channel Selectivity: Specific channels are designed for ions like Na+, K+, or Ca2+, ensuring precise control of membrane potential.
  • Synaptic Integration: Neurons process excitatory and inhibitory inputs through temporal and spatial summation to determine firing outcomes.
