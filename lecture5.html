Key Concepts

  • Seizure Classification: Partial seizures originate in specific brain regions, while generalized seizures involve both hemispheres. Absence seizures are a subtype characterized by brief lapses in consciousness.
  • EEG Patterns: Generalized seizures show synchronized activity across the cortex, while partial seizures have focal discharges. EEG waveforms help differentiate seizure types.
  • Anti-Epileptic Drugs (AEDs): AEDs target Na⁺ channels (e.g., phenytoin), T-type Ca²⁺ channels (e.g., ethosuximide), and GABAergic signaling (e.g., benzodiazepines) to control hyperexcitability.
  • Paroxysmal Depolarizing Shifts (PDS): These bursts of prolonged depolarization are hallmarks of epileptiform activity, driven by excitatory inputs and diminished inhibition.
  • Tonic-Clonic Seizures: Characterized by muscle stiffening (tonic phase) followed by rhythmic jerking (clonic phase), caused by widespread neuronal firing.
  • Absence Seizures: Thalamic T-type Ca²⁺ channels play a key role in generating 3 Hz spike-and-wave discharges seen in absence seizures.
  • Neuroinflammation: Chronic inflammation can exacerbate epileptic activity by disrupting the E/I balance and promoting excitatory signaling.
  • Epilepsy Pathophysiology: Loss of inhibitory control and excessive excitatory drive underlie seizure initiation and propagation.
  • Epilepsy Comorbidities: Cognitive impairments, anxiety, and depression are common comorbidities, reflecting widespread brain network dysfunction.
  • Therapeutic Advances: New AEDs targeting synaptic vesicle proteins (e.g., SV2A modulators) and neuroinflammatory pathways show promise.
