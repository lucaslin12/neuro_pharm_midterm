Key Concepts

  • Dose-Response Curves: These curves illustrate the relationship between drug concentration and effect. They help categorize full agonists, partial agonists, and antagonists based on their maximum efficacy and potency.
  • Pharmacokinetics (ADME): Absorption, Distribution, Metabolism, and Excretion (ADME) determine a drug's bioavailability and duration in the body. Factors like liver enzyme activity and renal clearance play pivotal roles.
  • Competitive vs. Non-Competitive Antagonists: Competitive antagonists shift dose-response curves to the right, whereas non-competitive antagonists reduce maximal efficacy without shifting potency.
  • Agonist Potency: Potency is measured by the EC50 (concentration causing 50% maximal effect). Lower EC50 indicates higher potency.
  • Inverse Agonists: These drugs stabilize receptors in their inactive state, reducing activity below baseline levels.
  • Behavioral Assays: Tests like the Tail Flick (pain threshold) and Open Field (anxiety/motor activity) evaluate pharmacological effects on behavior.
  • Prodrug Strategies: Prodrugs improve pharmacological profiles by enhancing solubility or targeting specific tissues for activation.
  • First-Pass Metabolism: Liver metabolism reduces drug bioavailability, necessitating higher oral doses compared to intravenous administration.
  • Therapeutic Index (TI): TI compares the effective dose (ED50) to the lethal dose (LD50), indicating drug safety margins.
  • Drug-Drug Interactions: Enzyme inhibitors or inducers can alter drug metabolism, leading to toxicity or reduced efficacy.
